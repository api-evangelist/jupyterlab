<h3>Exploring Petabytes of the Night Sky — Jupyter Notebooks at NOIRLab’s Astro Data Lab Science Platform</h3><p><em>By Robert Nikutta &amp; Stéphanie Juneau (NSF NOIRLab)</em></p><p>Imagine querying 420+ billion rows of astronomical catalog data — spanning 30 major sky surveys, observed over decades with telescopes on three continents — from a Jupyter notebook in your browser in seconds. No download. No HPC allocation request. No waiting.</p><p>That is what 4,800+ astronomers in over 90 countries can do every day at the <a href="https://datalab.noirlab.edu">Astro Data Lab</a> science platform. Data Lab is operated by <a href="https://noirlab.edu">NSF NOIRLab</a>, the National Optical-Infrared Astronomy Research Laboratory, headquartered in Tucson, Arizona, with observatories in Arizona, Hawai’i, and Chile. Since its public launch in June 2017, Astro Data Lab has quietly become one of the largest deployments of Jupyter notebooks in professional science — and a case study in what happens when you bring the compute to the data instead of the other way around.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*bllZBblDcMeLN2fxIS9PsA.png" /><figcaption>World map of 2024 number of data queries at Astro Data Lab by country (log-scale color): 64.2 million queries from 72 countries that year.</figcaption></figure><h3>The Data Problem Astronomy Had to Solve</h3><p>Modern sky surveys are data machines. The <a href="https://www.darkenergysurvey.org/">Dark Energy Survey</a> cataloged 690 million objects. <a href="https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/Gaia_overview">Gaia</a> measured positions and motions for 1.8 billion stars. The <a href="https://www.legacysurvey.org">DESI Legacy Surveys</a> cover 20,000 square degrees, nearly half of the full sky, in three optical bands. And the upcoming Rubin Observatory’s <a href="https://rubinobservatory.org/explore/how-rubin-works/lsst">Legacy Survey of Space and Time</a> (LSST) will generate roughly 10 million transient alerts <em>per night</em> starting later this year.</p><p>Traditional astronomy workflows begin with downloading relevant data to a local computer and to use locally installed specialized software tools to process and analyze the data. However, downloading these catalogs to a local machine is now often physically impossible. A single survey’s measurements table can exceed the combined disk space of an entire research group. And even if you could download it, the computing resources needed to query it efficiently at scale requires infrastructure most astronomers don’t have.</p><p>The answer the community converged on, like many industries dealing with big data: bring the compute to the data. Host the catalogs in databases, co-locate a computing environment next door, and give scientists a familiar interface to work in. That interface, increasingly, is a Jupyter notebook.</p><h3>Astro Data Lab: Jupyter at the Observatory</h3><p>Astro Data Lab was conceived in 2014 and went public in June 2017, originally built to support data releases from the Dark Energy Survey — a few terabytes of catalogs and tens of terabytes of images. We imagined a ceiling of roughly 500 users. We were wrong, in the best way.</p><p>Today the platform hosts:</p><ul><li><strong>About 420 billion catalog rows</strong> across 30+ major astronomical surveys (DES, Legacy Surveys, DESI, NOIRLab Source Catalog, SDSS, Gaia, unWISE, SMASH, S-PLUS, VHS, 2MASS, and dozens more)</li><li><strong>31 million spectra</strong> via SPARCL, our spectral access service (DESI DR1+EDR, SDSS/BOSS DR17)</li><li>Petabytes of images, accessible through a <a href="https://www.ivoa.net/documents/SIA/">Simple Image Access</a> service and cutout API</li><li><strong>Over 4,800 registered users</strong> from over 90 countries, who submit tens of millions of data queries each year</li></ul><p>Every registered user gets a persistent JupyterHub environment with the full astronomy Python stack pre-loaded — Astropy, NumPy, SciPy, Matplotlib, Pandas, Scikit-learn — and our own astro-datalab client library. The library provides core services, for instance auth and DB queries:</p><pre>from dl import authClient, queryClient<br />from getpass import getpass<br /><br /># Log in<br />token = authClient.login(input(&quot;username: &quot;),getpass(&quot;password: &quot;))<br /><br /># Query 10 objects from the NOIRLab Source Catalog near a sky position<br /># Right Ascension (RA) = 150.12 degrees<br /># Declination (Dec) = 2.21 degrees<br /># Search radius = 0.05 degrees<br /># q3c (Quad Tree Cube) is a spatial indexing scheme for Postgres<br /># gmag and rmag are the g-band and r-band magnitudes of objects<br /># in the NOIRLab Source Catalog Data Release 2, ‘object’ table.<br />result = queryClient.query(<br />    &quot;&quot;&quot;SELECT ra, dec, gmag, rmag FROM nsc_dr2.object<br />       WHERE q3c_radial_query(ra, dec, 150.12, 2.21, 0.05) LIMIT 10&quot;&quot;&quot;,<br />    fmt=&quot;pandas&quot;<br />)</pre><p>It’s as simple as that. The query runs on the database server next to the data; only the result set crosses the network.</p><h3>Use Case 1 — Seeing the Sky Inside a Notebook with AladinLite</h3><p>One of the most immediate joys of working with astronomical data is visualization: not just numbers in a table, but <em>where things are in the sky</em>, what the images look like, and how your query results relate to the underlying survey footprint.</p><p>We’ve integrated <a href="https://aladin.cds.unistra.fr/AladinLite/">AladinLite v3</a> — the interactive sky atlas from Centre de Données Astronomiques de Strasbourg (CDS) — directly into the notebook environment via the <a href="https://github.com/cds-astro/ipyaladin">ipyaladin</a> widget. With a handful of lines, astronomers can embed a fully interactive sky viewer in a notebook cell or next to their notebook in a “sidecar”, and overlay their own data on top of real survey imagery:</p><pre>import time<br />from astropy import units as u<br />from astropy.table import Table<br />from astropy.coordinates import SkyCoord<br />from ipyaladin import Aladin<br />from sidecar import Sidecar<br /><br /># Instantiate the Aladin interactive sky viewer <br />aladin = Aladin(full_screen=True)<br />with Sidecar(title=&quot;aladin_output&quot;,anchor='split-right'):<br />    display(aladin)<br /><br /># globular cluster NGC 1851 (RA, Dec)<br />aladin.target = SkyCoord(78.52809*u.deg, -40.04656*u.deg)<br />aladin.coo_frame = &quot;ICRSd&quot;  # set coordinate frame to ICRS, angles in deg<br />time.sleep(1)  # race condition<br />aladin.fov = 0.4  # set field of view to 0.4 degrees<br /><br /># Overlay catalog query results as circle markers<br />t = Table.from_pandas(df)  # e.g., from a previous query around NGC 1851<br />aladin.add_table(t,shape='circle',source_size=15,color='green')</pre><p>The result is a pannable, zoomable sky viewer — right in the notebook — with your query results overlaid as green circles on the actual sky image of a globular cluster (see figure below). Users can overlay MOCs (Multi-Order Coverage maps, which encode survey footprints), user-generated catalogs from a prior query, or any Virtual Observatory-standard data source.</p><p>This capability turns what was once a static plot into an exploratory tool: zoom into a cluster, click on a source, cross-match on the fly. For students and scientists unfamiliar with a dataset, it is often the fastest path from “I have a list of objects” to “I understand where they are and what I’m looking at.”</p><p><a href="https://github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest/blob/master/04_HowTos/Aladin/ipyaladin_MOC.ipynb">AladinLite integration is now active in our notebook library</a>, with full deployment into the new Data Lab Web Portal on the roadmap for later this year.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/822/1*3kJd4UCGCp0IpTrcL7fxqA.png" /><figcaption><a href="https://github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest/blob/master/04_HowTos/Aladin/ipyaladin_globular_cluster.ipynb"><em>AladinLite v3 sky viewer inside a Jupyter notebook</em></a><em>, showing a globular cluster with catalog query results in the outskirts of the cluster overlaid as green circles.</em></figcaption></figure><h3>Use Case 2 — Stacking Galaxy Spectra with SPARCL</h3><p>Spectroscopy — measuring how much light a star or a galaxy emits at each wavelength — is one of astronomy’s most powerful tools. But individual spectra are often noisy. The signal-to-noise ratio of a single optical spectrum for a faint galaxy can be too low to measure the emission lines that encode star formation rate, gas chemical content (Oxygen, Nitrogen, etc.) or the even more subtle absorption lines that create small wiggles in the shape of the spectrum, yet encapsulate crucial information such as the mass and age of the stars making up a galaxy.</p><p>One trick that astronomers have used for decades: combining or “stacking” spectra. Average hundreds of spectra together, and the noise level reduces while the signal builds up. What was invisible in a single spectrum becomes unmistakable in the stack. While the concept is simple, reading and manipulating large numbers of spectra can be time consuming or cumbersome.</p><p><a href="https://astrosparcl.datalab.noirlab.edu">SPARCL</a> (SPectra Analysis and Retrievable Catalog Lab) makes this possible at scale directly in a notebook. With `sparclclient`, users can currently search 31 million spectra by redshift range, target type, and survey, then retrieve flux arrays and wavelength grids ready for stacking:</p><pre>from sparcl.client import SparclClient<br /><br /># Instantiate the SPARCL client (connected to production server)<br />client = SparclClient()<br /><br /># Find SDSS spectra of galaxies in a redshift slice 0.1&lt;z&lt;0.3<br />found = client.find(<br />    outfields=['sparcl_id', 'ra', 'dec', 'redshift', 'spectype'],<br />    constraints={'spectype': ['GALAXY'],<br />                 'redshift': [0.1, 0.3],<br />                 'data_release': ['SDSS-DR17']},<br />)<br /><br /># Retrieve flux, wavelength, and inverse-variance arrays<br />retrieved = client.retrieve(found.ids,<br />            include=['flux', 'wavelength', 'ivar'])</pre><p>In our <a href="https://github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest/blob/master/03_ScienceExamples/SpectralStacking/SpectralStacking_SDSS.ipynb">SpectralStacking_SDSS</a> science example notebook, users first stack a small number of galaxy spectra (N=5) in eight bins of astrophysical color <em>g</em>−<em>r</em> (green and red filters), revealing trends from blue spectra with emission lines to red spectra with absorption lines but with noisy spectra. Then users stack hundreds of galaxy spectra for the same bins of color <em>g</em>−<em>r</em> and obtain much cleaner spectra where the small wiggles are now real astrophysical features and no longer buried in the noise.</p><p>The spectral rainbows below — N=5 then N=200 stacked galaxy spectra color-coded in bins of astrophysical color <em>g</em>−<em>r</em> — are each a single output cell from this notebook, generated entirely within the Data Lab environment.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*NZpW8VO92rpjjqyR47BywQ.png" /><figcaption>SPARCL spectral rainbows: top panel shows N=5 galaxy spectra stacked and color-coded by g−r color, spanning wavelengths 3750–6450 Ångstrom. Bottom panel shows the same exercise but with N=200 galaxy spectra per bin, greatly enhancing the signal-to-noise ratio.</figcaption></figure><h3>Use Case 3 — Variable Stars and the Coming Flood of Time-Domain Data</h3><p>Not all astronomical data is a static snapshot of the sky. Many of the most scientifically rich phenomena — pulsating stars, transiting exoplanets, exploding supernovae, gravitational lensing events — reveal themselves through <em>change</em> over time.</p><p>Among the most useful calibration tools in astrophysics are RR Lyrae stars: old, low-mass stars that pulsate with periods of 0.2–1 day and a brightness variation that traces their distance. Finding and characterizing them across millions of square degrees of sky requires querying multi-epoch photometry catalogs, computing period statistics, and folding light curves — all tasks that fit naturally in a notebook workflow.</p><p>Our <a href="https://github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest/blob/master/03_ScienceExamples/TimeSeriesAnalysisRrLyraeStar/TimeSeriesAnalysisOfRrLyraeStar.ipynb">TimeSeriesAnalysisRrLyraeStar</a> notebook demonstrates the full pipeline: query the SMASH DR2 catalog for stars with high photometric variability, run a Lomb-Scargle periodogram on the light curve, identify the dominant period, and phase-fold the observations to reveal the characteristic sawtooth pulsation profile:</p><pre>from astropy.timeseries import LombScargle<br />import numpy as np<br /><br />ls = LombScargle(t, y)  # time and magnitude from a previous query<br />frequency, power = ls.autopower()<br />period = 1./frequency # period is the inverse of frequency<br />best_period = period[np.argmax(power)]<br />phase = (t / best_period) % 1  # folded timeseries = light curve</pre><p>The resulting phase-folded light curve shown in the figure below is clean, precise, and immediately recognizable to any variable-star astronomer — produced entirely from archival survey data without a single new observation.</p><p>This kind of workflow is also a proving ground for the upcoming <a href="https://rubinobservatory.org/">Vera C. Rubin Observatory</a>’s <a href="https://rubinobservatory.org/explore/how-rubin-works/lsst">Legacy Survey of Space and Time</a> (LSST). When Rubin begins operations and delivers 10 million nightly alerts, the only workflows that will scale are ones already designed to run against large databases or specialized file systems, in shared computing environments, with notebook-native tooling. Astro Data Lab users are building those workflows today.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1000/1*JDuoMo_2SgPJ-b_md9nslg.png" /><figcaption>Phase-folded RR Lyrae light curve from the TimeSeriesAnalysisRrLyraeStar notebook, showing characteristic sawtooth pulsation with a 0.65 day period.</figcaption></figure><h3>The Notebook Ecosystem</h3><p>The three use cases above are drawn from our library of <strong>80+ open-source Jupyter notebooks</strong> at <a href="https://github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest">github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest</a>. The pilot framework was developed by two summer students working with the team.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*SvI9n6mkoQ5th35aClATWQ.png" /><figcaption>Footprints of 24 sky survey datasets hosted at Astro Data Lab. This opens deep learning and large-scale ML workflows in the same notebook environment where the data lives.</p><p><strong>An AI assistant.</strong> Our first-ever user survey, conducted in September 2025, ranked an in-notebook AI assistant as one of the top requested features. We are actively exploring what responsible, science-aware AI assistance looks like in this context — helping users construct SQL/ADQL queries, navigate datasets, and debug notebook code, without hallucinating catalog column names. jupyter-ai might come in very handy here.</p><p><strong>New integrated Web Portal.</strong> Our Data Explorer — an integrated web interface combining catalog browsing, query execution, image cutouts, spectral search, and job status monitoring — was rolled out last year. Some of the next milestones include integration of AladinLite into the portal, bringing the sky-visualization capability described above out of the notebook and into the browser-native interface, and a new integrated positional cross-matching service.</p><h3>Try It</h3><p>The full notebook library is open-source: <a href="https://github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest/">github.com/astro-datalab/notebooks-latest</a>.
